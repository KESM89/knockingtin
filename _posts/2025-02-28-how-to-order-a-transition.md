I get calls every week from field techs ordering transitions. Good techs, experienced techs — guys who've been in the trade for years. And a solid chunk of those calls end the same way: I have to call them back because I don't have enough information to cut the piece.

This isn't a knock on field techs. It's a communication problem, and it's one that costs everybody time. The fabricator has to stop, call back, wait for a return call, and restart the order. The tech has to track down measurements they thought they didn't need. The job sits.

Here's how to avoid it.

## What a Transition Actually Is (So We're Talking About the Same Thing)

A transition is a fitting that changes from one duct size to another. It might change both dimensions (24x12 to 16x10), or it might change one dimension while the other stays constant (24x12 to 24x8). It can be centered, offset to one side, or offset in two directions at once.

The confusion usually starts here, because "transition" gets used loosely in the field to mean several things. Make sure you and your fabricator are talking about the same fitting before you get into measurements.

## The Five Numbers You Need

**1. The starting size.** Width and height of the larger (or incoming) end. Width is always the horizontal dimension, height is the vertical. Don't guess — measure the existing duct if you're matching into something in the field.

**2. The ending size.** Width and height of the smaller (or outgoing) end. Same convention.

**3. The length.** How long does the transition need to be? This matters more than most techs realize. A short transition has steeper taper angles, which creates more turbulence and static pressure loss. SMACNA recommends a 15-degree maximum taper angle for good performance. If you need a short transition and can live with the pressure penalty, fine — but tell your fabricator the length and let them flag it if it's going to cause problems.

**4. The offset.** Is the transition centered, or does it need to be offset to one side? A centered transition distributes the taper evenly on all four sides. An offset transition is skewed so one face is square (or nearly so) and the taper is concentrated on the opposite faces. This is common when you're transitioning into a unit or fitting that's not centered on the duct.

**5. The connection type.** How does each end connect to what it's joining? Slip connection, drive cleats, flanged? And which gauge material?

## The One That Gets Skipped Most Often

Offset. Techs remember the four sizes and the length, and then describe a centered transition when what they actually need is an offset piece. The two fittings are not interchangeable — an offset transition installed where a centered one was fabricated either won't fit or will create alignment problems downstream.

If you're not sure whether you need an offset, take a photo of both ends of the space where the transition will go and send it along with your measurements. A photo is worth three phone calls.

## A Note on Direction

When you give width and height, be consistent about orientation. Width is horizontal when you're looking at the duct face straight-on. Height is vertical. This seems obvious until you're lying on your back in a crawlspace trying to measure a fitting above your head, and suddenly nothing feels obvious anymore.

When in doubt, describe where you're standing and which way is up. Your fabricator will sort it out.

## The Fastest Way to Order

If you want to make your fabricator's day, show up to the call with:

- A quick sketch (phone photo of a napkin drawing is fine) showing both ends and the direction of offset if there is one
- All five numbers written down before you dial
- A clear sense of how long you have before you need the piece

That's it. Clean information in, clean piece out, job keeps moving.
